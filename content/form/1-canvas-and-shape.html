As we begin our journey into the field of graphic design, it's important to start at the very beginning. Graphic design is the art of using form and color to successfully convey a message, but this is not as easy as it sounds. The design process is one of trial and error, and it often takes many iterations to create a design that clearly communicates what you're trying to say. Although we tend to think of this design process as something involving gut instinct, experienced designers have an ever-expanding checklist of visual relationships that helps guide them through to the end.

The first item on this checklist is learning how to manipulate a single shape to produce desired outcomes. We will investigate how changing three simple variables of a rectangle – the position, size, and rotation – can result in drastically different expressions. Although somehow basic, these variables are an important first set of techniques to be employed at the beginning of every design process, replacing hours of searching through online portfolios of the latest trends in the design world.

Size

The size of a shape is important, as it communicates whether that shape is important or not. By increasing the size of important shapes and decreasing the size of less important shapes, you can bring clarity to a design, and this is a pattern we encounter daily in everything from traffic signs to website dialog boxes.

In his famous advertising designs for Volkswagen, William Bernbach used the size of a car to emphasize the supporting text copy. This is a great example of a simple but highly effective design using sizing as a guiding principle.

It can often help to draw black boxes on top of actual designs to reveal this relationship, and doing it on work by famous designers will reveal how this is an integral part of most design processes.

Position

Another important relationship is the positioning of shapes in the canvas. Most languages have a natural reading direction (english is left to right, arabic right to left) that can be used to prioritize the importance of shapes. Proper use of positioning simply gives the reader a clue about where to start.

This pattern is used throughout the design world to create, in lack of a better word, rhythm. Graphic novels use the positioning of captions to guide the reader through the storyline. Newspapers place the most important text in a heading at the top of an article, which also tends to have a larger size to set it apart.

When you change the position of a shape, you also change the empty space between the shapes. This concept, often called whitespace, is another thing to keep in mind. In example X2, there is an equal amount of whitespace on the sides of the rectangle. This creates symmetry and balance, which is broken in example X3 where the square moves off center.

Rotation

A third important relationship relates to the rotation of shapes. Rotation can be used to make shapes come alive, as the whitespace around them becomes more complex and breaks the symmetry of the design.

Rotation is used in many types of design products: Children's books use rotated shapes to make them look fun and playful. Popular magazines use rotated grid systems to make the content look less dry, while publications for a narrow target audience use horizontal grid systems to imply quality of the content within.

EXAMPLE: Paul Rand uses squares with different rotations to make a playful cover for a children's book.

EXAMPLE: Josef muller brockman: 45 degree rotations. streamlined and digital.

In code, we define rotation as a number between 0 and 360 (degrees), often converted to a number between 0 and 6.28318530718 (radians) with the radians() function. It is important to understand how graphic programming languages – and P5.js in particular – applies rotation to shapes. When you use the rotate() function, you are actually rotating the entire coordinate system, and all shapes drawn after this rotation will be drawn in the rotated coordinate system.

Combining the three

These variables are all proportional to the dimensions of the canvas, so if you make the canvas bigger, the shapes should be bigger too. This would take a lot of manual work in a program like Illustrator, but in code we can use the width and height of the canvas to automatically calculate the position of our shapes.

The following two lines of code have identical outcomes, but the latter will place the rectangle in the bottom right corner, no matter the canvas size. The former will not.

{% include code_static.html caption="Do not use hardcoded numbers." class="media-width dont" code="rect(900, 900, 100, 100);" %} {% include code_static.html caption="Do use width and height to calculate position." class="media-width do" code="rect(width - 100, height - 100, 100, 100);" %}

It's common to find problems in existing designs that can be traced back to the use of position, size, and rotation. Whether it's a user interface that fails to emphasize an important button, or a graph with ambiguity around its underlying dataset, it's clear that some designers rush to make things pretty without thinking about these basic relationships. This should be seen as a failure of design, and one is much better off including these ideas as a vital part of any design process.
